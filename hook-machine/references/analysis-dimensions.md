How to analyze a set of winning hooks against losing ones. The output of this analysis is a set of **contrasts** (what winners do that losers don't), which become the custom principles in the rubric.

Always analyze as a contrast. A trait only matters if it separates winners from losers. "Winners are specific" is useless if losers are specific too. "Winners name a number in the first 6 words; losers describe a category" is a usable principle.

---

## 1. Psychology

The emotional mechanism that fires in the reader's first second.

Look for:
- **Curiosity gap** — does the hook open a loop the reader needs closed? Where exactly is the gap?
- **Tension / stakes** — is something at risk, contested, or surprising?
- **Recognition** — does it name a pain or situation the reader is already living?
- **Payoff promise** — is there a concrete reward for reading on, stated up front?

Winner-vs-loser questions: Do winners promise a specific payoff in sentence one while losers bury it? Do winners create a gap while losers state a complete, closed thought (nothing left to learn)?

## 2. Trigger words & framing

The specific lexical choices, not the structure.

Look for:
- Recurring **words** in winners that are absent in losers (verbs, intensifiers, concrete nouns).
- **Framing** moves: contrarian reframe, comparison, before/after, named enemy, constraint.
- Vague superlatives ("amazing", "game-changing", "the best") — usually a loser tell.

Winner-vs-loser questions: Which 5-10 words show up disproportionately in winners? Do winners use concrete, sensory, or numeric language while losers use abstractions?

> This is the dimension most prone to false transfer. The trigger words you extract are valid **only for this owner's audience.** Never seed them from another creator. Re-derive on each refresh.

## 3. Grammar / structure

Sentence construction, independent of meaning.

Look for:
- **Declarative vs. hedged.** Winners tend to state things as fact; losers hedge or ask soft questions.
- **Payoff position.** Winners front-load the payoff; losers delay it behind throat-clearing ("In this post I want to talk about...").
- **Length and rhythm.** Are winners tighter? Where does the first sentence break?
- **The two-sentence amplifier** — claim, then amplify with a comparison or reframe in sentence two.

Winner-vs-loser questions: Is the payoff in the first 8 words for winners and after word 15 for losers? Do winners avoid opening with a dependent clause?

## 4. Non-obvious

Patterns only visible by reading the full text — the things metrics-only tools miss. This is where the edge is.

Look for:
- Openers that quietly **kill** performance (e.g., leading with a quote/clip reference instead of the owner's own framing; opening on "I" instead of "you").
- Identity / branding-only openers that assume the reader already cares.
- Topic-format mismatches (see below).
- Anything that surprised you in the data — a winner that "shouldn't" have won, or vice versa.

Winner-vs-loser questions: Is there a structural opener that appears only in losers? Is there a subtle move (a specific second-sentence pattern) that appears only in winners?

## Quick loss-diagnosis lens: the 4 failure modes

A fast way to label *why* a loser lost. Every weak opener fails at one of four points (full craft treatment lives in the `writing` skill's `references/hooks.md`). Use this as shorthand on top of the four dimensions above — it consolidates several of the loser tells into one checklist:

| Failure mode | The loser tell | Maps to dimension |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Delay** | Topic buried behind throat-clearing; payoff lands after word 15 | Grammar / structure (payoff position) |
| **Confusion** | Long words, passive voice, multi-clause first sentence | Grammar / structure + trigger words |
| **Irrelevance** | Opens on "I/me" or a topic, not "you" or a lived pain point | Non-obvious ("I" vs "you" tell) |
| **Disinterest** | Clear and relevant, but opens no contrast / curiosity gap | Psychology (curiosity gap) |

When a loser commits one, name the failure mode in the contrast — "winners front-load the topic; losers delay it behind a setup line" is a usable, gradeable principle. The two outcomes the rubric is ultimately scoring for: **topic clarity** and **on-target curiosity** — a hook that nails both rarely lands in the loser set.

## Format-topic fit screen

A hook format that won on one topic can flop on another, because the psychology, format, and even grammar don't transfer cleanly. Before reusing a winning format for a new topic, run all three tests. Any fail = cut the format.

1. **Structural compatibility.** Does the mad-lib structure actually accept this topic? If the format needs a metric or proof point and the topic is conceptual with no external data, the substitution forces you to invent something fake. Fail.
2. **Tone match.** Does the format's energy match the topic's? A warning/urgency format on an inspirational topic feels forced. Fail.
3. **Word-substitution test (the most important filter).** Drop the topic's key terms into the mad-lib slots and read it out loud. If the result has awkward phrasing, grammatical friction, or makes the reader re-read, it fails — no matter how well it scored structurally.

Quick reference for common formats:

| Format | Needs | Don't use when |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Stat / case-study opener ("I studied 200 X...") | Real external data or a concrete result | The topic is conceptual / a framework with no stat to anchor |
| Vulnerable / confession opener | A genuine personal stake | The topic is purely technical or impersonal |
| Contrarian-take opener | A real piece of conventional wisdom to push against | There's no established consensus to invert |
| Numbered-outcome opener ("X result in Y time") | A concrete, quantifiable outcome | The outcome is vague or can't be measured |

Forcing a proven format onto the wrong topic is a top reason a "should-have-worked" hook falls flat.

## Boosted-post screen

When ranking by reach or views, paid/promoted posts contaminate the data. Detect them by the mismatch between reach and engagement:

- A post in the top tier by reach but in the **bottom tier by engagement rate** was likely boosted.
- Flag it, exclude it from the winner set, and note the exclusion.

Always prefer **engagement rate** (engagements / impressions) over raw reach as the ranking metric. It is far harder to fake and reflects whether the hook actually worked.
