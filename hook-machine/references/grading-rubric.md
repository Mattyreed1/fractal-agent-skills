# Grading Rubric

The always-on baseline for grading any hook, plus how to grade. Layered on top of this at runtime: the data-derived custom principles + trigger-word bank from Mode 1 (calibrated to the owner). Generation craft (how to *write* a hook) lives in the `writing` skill's `references/hooks.md`; this file is how to *evaluate* one.

A "hook" is the opener of any piece (post, thread, video script, email subject). Where the wording says "reader," read "reader or viewer."

## The 8 Universal Hook Principles

Always active, every hook, every niche.

1. **Rapid Context.** What the piece is about must be clear in the first sentence. The reader can't opt into value they can't see coming. If the topic isn't clear by the end of sentence one, the hook is failing.
2. **Clarity / Comprehension.** Zero ambiguity. If the hook can be read two ways, rewrite it. Comprehension loss (the reader misreads or stalls on the words) is the #1 silent killer of hooks.
3. **Contrast / Curiosity Loop.** The strongest lever. Contrast is the distance between what the reader currently believes and what you're asserting. The bigger the gap on a topic they care about, the harder the pull. Stated (name the common belief, then counter it) or implied (introduce something that breaks the assumed baseline).
4. **Distillation.** Fewest words possible. Every word earns its place. If cutting a word doesn't cost clarity or impact, cut it. The hook is the most valuable real estate in the piece.
5. **Specificity.** Numbers, names, timeframes, concrete outcomes. "3 things" beats "a few things"; "30 days" beats "quickly." Specificity turns an abstract promise into a tangible one.
6. **Absorption Rate.** Can the reader process the hook in a single pass at the speed they consume the feed (a skim for written, speaking speed for spoken)? Front-loaded jargon, too many ideas, or tangled sentence structure all kill it. There's no second read in the feed. Plain language first; technical terms only after priming.
7. **Instant Value Promise.** The value IS the hook, not a tease that needs more consumption just to understand. If the reader has to go further only to learn what the piece is about, the hook is broken.
8. **Credibility Anchor (bonus).** A proof point in the next line(s): a personal result, a case study, a stat, a trusted source. Not required (many hooks win on curiosity or contrast alone), but when present and natural it lifts willingness to stay. Don't penalize its absence; reward it when done well.

## Anti-patterns (screen for these)

Flag any of these when grading, and never produce them when generating:

- **Vague superlatives without specifics** — "the most powerful," "a genius trick." Unverifiable, and the reader knows it.
- **Delayed topic context** — the topic isn't clear until sentence 2+. Everything before clarity is bounce-fuel.
- **Confusing sentence logic** — phrasing that can be read multiple ways.
- **Throat-clearing openers** — "In my opinion," "So basically," "I want to talk about." Wasted prime real estate.
- **Crammed points** — multiple disconnected ideas teased at once instead of one clear promise.
- **Jargon on a cold brain** — insider terms before the reader is primed to understand them.
- **Generic fear kickers** — "and if you don't, you'll fail." Bolts onto any topic; does no concept-specific work.
- **Em-dashes in the hook** — never use an em-dash (—) in a generated or rewritten hook; it reads as AI-written. Use a period, comma, or line break. If a user's hook contains one, flag it.

## Grading methodology

Holistic, not mechanical. The principles are the framework, but grades are not assigned by counting how many are satisfied. Some matter more depending on the topic: a conceptual topic may not allow tight numeric specificity, and that alone doesn't drop a grade if contrast and clarity are exceptional.

### Grade scale

| Grade | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **A+** | All applicable principles firing, no meaningful tradeoffs. Best possible hook for this topic. |
| **A** | Nearly all strong. Minor tradeoff that doesn't materially hurt. |
| **A-** | Strong on most. One identifiable, fixable weakness. |
| **B+** | Good, would perform. 1-2 clear improvement areas. |
| **B** | Functional. Passes the basics, misses opportunities. |
| **B-** | Mediocre. Right idea, multiple execution flaws. |
| **C** | Weak. Multiple core principles violated. Likely underperforms. |
| **D** | Broken. Fails fundamentals (no rapid context, clarity, or value promise). |
| **F** | Actively harmful. Confusing, misleading, or off-target. |

### Per-list thresholds

- **Generated originals (Mode 2, List B):** internally iterate — grade, rewrite anything below B+, repeat — until all are **B+ or above** before presenting. Never surface a sub-B+ hook you wrote.
- **Format-matched (Mode 2, List A):** a wider range is fine, but **cut anything below C** (it failed the compatibility screen). Show the grade on each.
- **User-submitted (Mode 3):** grade **honestly**, do not inflate and do not reject. The value is accurate feedback. Always follow with rewrites.
