# Intros

You write credible introductions that establish trust and engage readers with effective rhythm patterns.

## The Credibility Question

**Why should the reader trust me?**

Ask yourself:
- Why am I the right person to write about this topic?
- Should they trust me because of my Personal Credibility?
- Or because of my Borrowed Credibility?

## Readers Aren't Picky (But They Need a Reason)

You don't need to be a Harvard Graduate, Rocket Scientist, or political official for readers to trust you.

**Example**: Imagine a friend asks for a Friday Night Movie Recommendation. You say, "Watch this one." They ask, "Why?" You reply: "Because I've watched every single Rom Com movie on Netflix, and trust me, this one is the best."

That's all they need to hear. Your opinion is trusted.

**Writing is no different**: Readers need a reason to trust you.

## What Makes Someone Credible?

- Hours spent doing this thing?
- Meaningful milestone achieved?
- Years spent studying and practicing?
- What someone else with experience says to do?
- Wisdom from a very smart book by a very smart person?
- Time spent around others who've achieved the same thing?

## Give Readers a Reason to Trust You

Make a note in your introduction:

**"This is why you should trust what I'm about to tell you."**

### Examples
- "How do I know how to build a real estate empire? Because over the last 10 years, I've bought and sold over 50 different rental properties."
- "Why should you take my Netflix series recommendations? Because I've watched over 5,000 hours of Netflix shows and movies."
- "Why is this the best strategy to get distribution on your writing? Because I went and interviewed 20 different best-selling authors, and this is what they said to do."

## Personal Credibility vs Borrowed Credibility

### Personal Credibility
Sharing what you know based on your own personal experience.

Credibility can be small achievements or life milestones:
- "I am a new mom" (You had a kid! You're credible to anyone who doesn't have kids.)
- "I built an audience of 5,000 software developers on LinkedIn" (More than 0 = credible to anyone with less.)
- "I have overcome cancer" (Credible to anyone fighting cancer or hasn't had it.)

### Borrowed Credibility
Sharing what someone else knows based on their experience.

**Example**: You don't have to be a professional copywriter to share David Ogilvy's rules for successful copywriting. Your credibility doesn't matter—you're borrowing David Ogilvy's.

## Framework to Eliminate Imposter Syndrome

### If writing about something you've personally experienced:
Just tell the reader: "I'm just telling you what I experienced here."

That's all they need to trust you.

### If writing about something you don't have personal experience in:
"Borrow" someone else's credibility and share what THEY have to say about the topic.

## Before You Start Writing

Ask yourself:
- Are you writing this from a place of Personal Credibility?
- Or is this a topic better suited to Borrowed Credibility?

## Common Questions

**"What if I don't have any personal credibility?"**

Chances are, you do—you just don't realize it.

Personal credibility can be something as simple as: "I moved across the country." Great! You're credible to write about YOUR EXPERIENCE moving, lessons you learned, mistakes you made, things you'd do differently.

If you truly have no credibility on a topic:
a) Why are you writing about that topic?
b) Consider writing about it with Borrowed Credibility instead.

## Writing Rhythm for Intros: 1/3/1

The simplest 'rhythm writing' pattern is 1/3/1.

**Structure**:
- {1} Open with 1 short to medium-length sentence
- {3} Then, in three sentences, add more description and color
- {1} Finish the idea with one single sentence

**Example**:
```
{1} The simplest 'rhythm writing' pattern is what we like to call 1/3/1.

{3} This means you open with 1 short to medium-length sentence. Then, in three sentences you add more description and color. And then you finish the idea with one single sentence.

{1} Just like this section here.
```

### Pattern Breakdown
1/3/1 literally refers to the number of sentences, and the pattern they're stacked in:
- One strong opening sentence
- Three descriptive sentences, advancing your point forward
- One final concluding sentence

You can repeat 1/3/1 over and over again, all the way down the page.

## Expanding the Middle

If 1/3/1 doesn't give you enough space, "expand the middle."

Keep the same structure (single sentences to open and close), but add content to the middle:
- 1/4/1
- 1/5/1
- 1/6/1
- 1/7/1

The key: Maintain your single-sentence 'bookends' (opener and closer sentences). This gives the reader an easy entry point and rewards them with a single sentence at the end.

**Remember**: If you're going to expand your writing, expand the middle.
