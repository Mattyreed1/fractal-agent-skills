# Hooks

You write effective single-sentence openers that "hook" the reader/viewer's attention in the very first sentence.

## What every hook must do (and the 4 ways it dies)

Before you pick an opener, know the job. A hook has to deliver exactly two things:

1. **Topic clarity** — after the first line, the reader knows *precisely* what this is about. One subject, not three. A vague opener spawns several possible subjects; each reader chases a different one, and most churn when the next line doesn't answer the question in *their* head. They don't leave because they dislike you — they leave because the thread stopped making sense.
2. **On-target curiosity** — the reader believes it's *for them* and feels pulled to keep going.

Get both and the reader opts in. Miss either and they're gone. Before shipping a hook, screen it against the four ways it fails:

| Failure | What it looks like | Fix |
|---------|--------------------|-----|
| **Delay** | The topic arrives late; line one is throat-clearing ("This is the craziest thing I've ever seen…") | **Speed to value** — name the topic in the first few words. Every word before context bleeds readers. |
| **Confusion** | The reader can't parse what it means for them — long words, passive voice, too many clauses | Rewrite at a **5th–6th grade reading level**: fewer words, simpler words, active voice. If unsure, have an LLM "rewrite this at a sixth-grade reading level, same meaning." |
| **Irrelevance** | They get the topic but aren't sure it's for *them* | Write **"you/your," not "I/me."** Anchor to a **pain point (need-to-have)**, not a topic (nice-to-have). "Three trends in skincare" is a nice-to-have; "If you struggle with acne, try these three things" is a need-to-have. |
| **Disinterest** | Clear and relevant, but no pull | Open a curiosity loop with **contrast** (see Structural Patterns → Contrast). |

Gut check on any draft: *Does this reference one unmistakable subject? Does it plant one single question I'd want answered?* If either answer isn't a clear yes, rewrite before going further.

## The 6 Proven Single-Sentence Openers

1. Open with 1 strong, declarative sentence
2. Open with a thought-provoking question
3. Open with a controversial opinion
4. Open with a moment in time
5. Open with a vulnerable statement
6. Open with a weird, unique insight

## 1. Strong, Declarative Sentence

The key here is to not "hedge."

**Bad Example**: "Some people think cutting costs is the best way to save money. Others believe it's better to just focus on making more. But the truth is, both work."

**Why it's horrible**: The reader already KNOWS both approaches work. They want YOU to tell THEM which one is correct and why.

### Examples
- Becoming successful doesn't just "happen."
- The wealthiest people in the world have the simplest investment portfolios.
- Going to the gym isn't a hobby, it's a lifestyle.
- The key to effective project management isn't a tool or platform—it's your ability to listen.
- You can't change the world without first changing yourself.

## 2. Thought-Provoking Question

What question is your target reader already asking themselves?

What's going through their head? What do they want to know more about? Where are they stuck? What keeps them up at night?

When you open with a question your target reader is already asking (or doesn't know they should be asking), you meet them exactly where they are—and they feel understood.

**Caveat: declaratives usually beat questions.** A question is the higher-risk opener. A generic or weak one reads as hedging. Use a question ONLY when it's one your reader is *already asking themselves*. If you're not sure it clears that bar, convert it to a confident declarative (technique #1). Rule of thumb: winners state that things *are*; losers ask whether they might be.

### Examples
- Did you know the average Millennial has less than $10,000 in their savings account?
- What's the difference between someone who goes pro versus someone who remains an amateur?
- Is there such a thing as eternal happiness?
- Is artistic talent innate? Or can it be taught?
- If you've been thinking about writing online, was your first impulse to start a blog?

## 3. Controversial Opinion

The key isn't to be controversial for controversy's sake.

Figure out what is conventional wisdom in your industry, and say the opposite.

**Example**: In online writing, conventional wisdom says "Start a blog." It's "controversial" to say, "Want to start writing online? DON'T START A BLOG."

### Examples
- It's impossible to be both rich and happy.
- The U.S. should not be involved in the war between Russia and Ukraine.
- If you drive a Tesla, you don't actually care about the environment—you just want people to think you do.
- Men and women should not make the same amount of money. Women should make more.
- Intelligence isn't something you're born with.

## 4. Moment in Time

This creative writing technique is incredibly effective.

Instead of just saying what happened, put a specific date, time, place, age, or price tag in front of it.

**Compare**:
- "There was a time when I was incredibly insecure."
- "When I was 16 years old, at the height of my popularity in high school, was when I was incredibly insecure."

The specificity of age and that "moment in time" pulls you in.

### Examples
- In 1982, David Ogilvy wrote an internal memo to the employees of his advertising agency titled "How to write."
- At 5:46 AM Pacific Time, September 11th, 2001, America changed forever.
- My fear of cafeteria trays began in 8th grade.
- When I was 17 years old, I became one of the highest-ranked World of Warcraft players in North America.

## 5. Vulnerable Statement

Vulnerability exists on a spectrum.

You don't have to air your dirty laundry or share things that make you uncomfortable. For some people, talking about getting fired is "vulnerable." For others, talking about a family member struggling with alcoholism doesn't feel vulnerable at all.

Figure out what level of vulnerability works for you.

Vulnerability of any kind pulls readers into your writing.

### Examples
- By the time I closed the doors of my first company, I'd lost more than $10 million of investor money.
- I've never been a very social person.
- For 27 years, I've struggled with alcoholism.
- The night before running my first marathon, I called my ex-girlfriend and told her I still loved her.
- For the first 10 years of my career, I was a terrible employee.
- I don't know anything about investing. Nada. Zilch.

## 6. Weird, Unique Insight

This technique is used all the time by viral documentaries.

The best documentaries always open with some weird, unique insight, stat, or study that makes you go, "Woah, I didn't know that."

### Examples
- Did you know that 85% of the world's oxygen comes from phytoplankton?
- California is not the largest state in the US. Alaska is.
- The average Millennial has less than $60,000 saved up in retirement by the time they reach age 40.
- Crows are one of the most intelligent animals on planet earth.
- My wife can boil a dozen eggs in 7 minutes or less.

## Structural Patterns

These apply *on top of* the 6 openers above. They shape how the opener lands, not which one you pick.

### Two-Sentence Amplifier

Don't stop at the claim. State it in sentence 1, then amplify in sentence 2 with a comparison, contrast, or contrarian reframe. The second sentence is where the hook earns attention.

- **Flat:** Most creators trigger one type of dopamine in a video.
- **Amplified:** Most creators trigger one type of dopamine in a video. The viral ones trigger six.

The amplifier turns a statement into a gap the reader needs closed. Pairs most naturally with the declarative (#1) and weird-insight (#6) openers.

### Outcome + Constraint Specificity

The most magnetic specificity names a concrete outcome *under a constraint*: a result paired with a limit on time, budget, or effort.

- "30 days of content in 30 minutes."
- "How I closed $1M in pipeline with one cold email."
- "Rebuilt the whole workflow in a single weekend."

The constraint is what makes the outcome feel earned and repeatable instead of lucky. Sharpens the "moment in time" (#4) and "weird insight" (#6) openers.

### Format-Topic Fit

A hook format that crushed on one topic can flop on another, because the psychology, format, and even grammar don't always transfer. Screen the format against the topic before you commit:

- **Stat / case-study openers** ("I studied 200 X...") need real external data. Don't force one onto a conceptual or framework piece, because there's no stat to anchor it.
- **Vulnerable / moment-in-time openers** need a genuine personal stake. Don't manufacture one for a purely technical topic.

If format and topic don't match, switch openers. Forcing a "proven" format onto the wrong topic is a top reason a hook falls flat.

### Contrast (the curiosity engine)

Curiosity isn't random — it's the *distance* between what the reader currently believes (A) and the better, faster, or cheaper alternative you offer (B). Naming that gap re-agitates the pain that A never solved, which creates the need to read on. No gap, no pull.

- **Stated contrast** — name both A and B: "Most people fight acne with Accutane. This herbal remedy works three times faster." Blunt, impossible to miss.
- **Implied contrast** — name only B and let the reader supply A: "This herbal supplement is eight times more effective." Works when the reader already knows the baseline options.

Pairs most naturally with the declarative (#1) and controversial-opinion (#3) openers.

### Lead With the Outcome (keep the character relatable)

For anything meant to *convert*, not just inform, open on the **dream outcome** the reader wants — not the problem they have. Stating the solved state is "one standard deviation away": it makes them picture having it and close the gap themselves, which pulls harder than restating a pain they already know.

Two requirements:
1. **A dream outcome** — what was, or could be, achieved.
2. **A relatable, constraint-free character** — you, the reader, or a third party they see themselves in. The instant the character looks like they had an unfair advantage ("…with my $1,200 setup"), the reader dismisses the outcome and scrolls. Frame the method as easy ("one simple step," "without doing anything crazy").

Five reliable frames for the outcome + character:
- **About me** — "I hit [outcome] using [easy method]."
- **If I** — "If I wanted [outcome], here's the one thing I'd do."
- **To you** — "If you want [outcome], use [method]."
- **Can you?** — "Is it possible to [outcome] under [tight constraint]?"
- **Third party** — "[Relatable person] just hit [outcome] — here's how."

This sharpens the "Outcome + Constraint Specificity" pattern above: that one nails the *number*; this one makes the reader the *protagonist*.

## How to Use Hooks

You can use single sentence openers for each section in a longer piece. Each new section warrants a new "opener."

If you have 3 main points in your piece, use one of these proven openers at the beginning of each main point to re-hook the reader's attention.
