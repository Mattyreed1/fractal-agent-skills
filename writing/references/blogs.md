# Blogs

You are an expert at formatting blog posts with subheads, writing rhythms, and visual structure that makes content easy to read and engaging.

## The Art of Subheads

Subheads are bolded sentences throughout a piece that help the reader know "where" they are in your line of thinking.

### Why Subheads Matter

Subheads allow readers to:
- Skim and jump around to find sections they're looking for
- See that content has been carefully constructed for their benefit
- Navigate both during and after reading

### How to Use Subheads

Some writers start by listing Main Points in bolded subheads first, then "coloring inside the lines" afterwards (called "Prepping The Page").

If you've already written content, split your essay equally into chunks.

**Example**: For a ~300-word Atomic Essay, every 100 words should be a bolded subhead (splitting into thirds).

### Why Even Splitting Works

1. **Visual Symmetry**: Creates a sense of balance that LOOKS easy and fun to read
2. **Scroll Milestones**: Readers can't scroll more than twice without seeing the next subhead
3. **Clarity**: Gives you clear boundaries—100 words to talk about X, then 100 words for Y

Subheads bring clarity.

### Common Questions

**"Won't subheads make my essay a listicle?"**

Yes and no. If you add numbers to subheads, it becomes a listicle (which isn't bad!). But just using bolded subheads doesn't make it a listicle. It does make it 100x easier for readers to skim and follow your train of thought.

**"How many subheads should I use?"**

Depends on length, but a good rule of thumb: Look at any given 'page' and divide it in half or thirds. Subheads should help it look symmetrical.

**"What should each subhead say?"**

Very simple. If your headline says you're giving "mistakes," each subhead should be Mistake #1, Mistake #2, etc. If your headline says "reasons," each subhead should be Reason #1, Reason #2, etc.

Your subheads are the fastest way for readers to decide whether you're delivering on the headline's promise.

**"What about platforms like X (Twitter) and LinkedIn where you can't bold?"**

Get creative! Use CAPS, $symbols$, even emojis to help readers understand where one section ends and another starts.

## Writing Rhythm: 1/3/1

### What is 1/3/1?

It's a technique for injecting rhythm into your writing without even needing to think about it.

1. Open with a single sentence (single-sentence opener)
2. Expand on the idea in three sentences
3. End with another sentence of conclusion

### Example

(1) What is 1/3/1?

(3) It's a technique for injecting rhythm into your writing without even needing to think about it. First you open with a single sentence. Then, you expand on the idea in three sentences.

(1) And then end with another sentence of conclusion.

If we didn't point out the 1/3/1 pattern, you probably wouldn't notice it. Yet, using 1/3/1 gives the feeling of fast and slow, short and long, alternating back and forth—which creates rhythm.

### Other Writing Rhythms

1/3/1 isn't the only rhythm:
- 1/4/1
- 1/5/1
- 1/6/1
- 1/2/5/2/1
- 1/2/3/5/3/2/1

**Key Pattern**: Most effective rhythms start with a single-sentence opener, crescendo up, then come back down and end with a single sentence.

This gives readers the feeling of starting & stopping individual thoughts/sections before reading the next idea.

### 1/3/1 + Subhead

Think of each subhead as the beginning of a new section—then inside that section is where you use these writing rhythms.

**Pattern**:
- Subhead
- 1/3/1
- Subhead
- 1/3/1

The subhead doesn't count as the first '1.' It's simply drawing a line between sections.

### Common Questions

**"Do I have to use the same writing rhythm all the way down?"**

No! Change it up: 1/3/1, subhead, 1/5/1, subhead, 1/2/5/2/1, etc.

**"How does this work for short-form content like Tweets?"**

Same idea, but instead of 3 long sentences in the middle, use 3 quick bullet points. The rhythm becomes 1/3 bullets/1.

**"If I use 1/3/1 in Atomic Essays it makes the essay too long. What do I do?"**

Adjust to the type of thing you're writing. For a Tweet, use 1/3 bullets/1. For an Atomic Essay, fit one or two 1/3/1 sequences. For an 800-word Medium article or 8,000-word essay, use 1/3/1 and other rhythms repeatedly.

## How to Create Rhythm & Musicality

Musicality and rhythm in writing is not difficult to create.

**It's all about sentence length.**

The key is alternating between long sentences and short sentences—ideally in some sort of pattern.

See? Short sentence, followed by medium sentence, followed by long sentence. If you pay attention as you read, you can 'hear' the pauses, the build-up, and the long crescendo of pace as sentences are processed in your brain.

This is all you have to do to make your writing 'flow.'

## Expanding The Middle

If 1/3/1 doesn't give you enough space to say what you want, expand the middle.

The secret: Keep the same 1/3/1 structure (single sentences to open and close), but add content to the middle part of the 'sandwich.'

**Examples**:
- 1/4/1
- 1/5/1
- 1/6/1
- 1/7/1

The structure is the same, but all that's changing is the length of the middle paragraph. Maintain your single-sentence 'bookends' (opener and closer sentences) to give readers easy entry and a rewarding single sentence at the end.

**If you're going to expand your writing, expand the middle.**

## Creating Rhythm Pyramids

The advanced level of 'expanding the middle' is creating Rhythm Pyramids.

Elongate your writing by creating new patterns to repeat—up to your big paragraph of description, then back down again.

### Why Pyramids Work

Each sentence (and stack of sentences) builds on the one before it:
- Single-sentence opener makes it feel 'easy' to read the next two sentences
- Those two sentences make it easy to dig into 5 sentences of description
- After that big paragraph, readers are tired from 'climbing the hill'
- They want downhill momentum, so bring it back down to two sentences
- Then a final single sentence—so relieving

### Effective Rhythm Pyramids

- 1/2/5/2/1
- 1/2/7/2/1
- 1/2/3/7/3/2/1

Notice: In all of these, the middle paragraph is longest, and the 'up' path and 'down' path mirror each other.

**Remember**: The whole secret to formatting and creating effective writing rhythms is creating & repeating patterns.

## Creating Punchy Rhythms

The last type of writing rhythm is creating 'punchy' sections.

Boom. Boom. Boom.

**Punchy Rhythms**:
- 1/1/1
- 2/2/1
- 3/2/1

These work best at:
- **Beginnings of sections**: Create immediate sense of momentum
- **Ends of sections**: Create feeling of strong conclusion

**Key**: Always end on 1 single sentence. Ending a section with 2 or 3 sentences feels like you stopped with no final resolution.

## Writing Rhythm Mistakes

### Bad Rhythms to Avoid

- 2/2/2/2/2
- 3/3/3/3/3
- 4/4/4/4/4
- 1/2/2/3/3/2/1
- 1/3/3/3/3/1

**Notice the pattern?**

The worst writing rhythms have minimal change in section length. Even worse: Multiple sections in a row with the exact same length.

Nothing makes writing sound more 'monotone' than multiple two or three sentence paragraphs in a row.

**Avoid these like the plague.**

### Think in Opposites

What you want: Small numbers to big numbers, back to small numbers:
- 1/3/1
- 1/5/1
- 1/7/1

The more dramatic the jump (between single-sentence paragraphs and multi-sentence paragraphs), the more your writing will 'sound' like it's moving.

Think of paragraphs like waves in the ocean. What makes them 'fun' is small waves, followed by a BIG wave, followed by small waves again.

This is the easiest upgrade you can make to your writing—and the effects on the reader will be immediate.

Your writing will 'sound' lovely.
