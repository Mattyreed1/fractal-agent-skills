# Worked Example: Hero's Journey (1st Person POV Problem Solution)

> Format reference: `references/storytelling.md` § 1.
> Medium: LinkedIn post (~180 words).
> Beats hit: Intro → Inflection → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Resolution/CTA.

---

Three years ago I was burning out as a junior dev. 12-hour days, weekends gone, and I still couldn't ship anything that didn't break in prod.

It got so bad I'd feel sick opening my laptop on Monday.

I tried better todo apps. Pomodoro. A $400/month productivity coach. Nothing stuck.

Then I read one line in an old engineering blog: "You don't have a productivity problem. You have a queue problem."

So I limited my work-in-progress to one ticket. Just one. No second tab, no parallel work.

Within six weeks I was shipping more than the rest of my team, working 8-hour days, and lifting again on Saturdays.

That's why I now run a 4-week WIP-limit cohort for engineers drowning in their own tabs.

Comment WIP if you want the playbook.

---

**Why this works:** every beat is present (problem → exaggerated pain → failed attempts → novel solution → result → CTA), the failed attempts are specific enough to be relatable, and the "novel solution" is one sentence the reader can repeat to themselves — which is what makes this format spread.
